Decision Making with Relevant Costs

The decision types you'll be tested on — each reduces to the same move: compare only the costs & revenues that change. All examples use the course's Gabriela & Co. data.

① One-time-only special order

Gabriela & Co. can make 44,000 towels/month but currently makes 30,000 → it has spare capacity. A hotel offers to buy 5,000 towels at £11.50 (= £57,500). No marketing costs apply to this order.

Variable manufacturing cost / towel£8.50 (DM 6.50 + DL 0.50 + mfg 1.50)
Full cost / towel (for reference)£20.50 (variable 13.50 + fixed 7.00) — includes irrelevant fixed & marketing
Relevant cost of the order£8.50 × 5,000 = £42,500
Incremental revenue£57,500 − £42,500 = £15,000 extra profit
Contribution / towel£11.50 − £8.50 = £3.00 × 5,000 = £15,000
Decision rule & answer

Accept if the revenue differential > the cost differential. Here yes (+£15,000). The £20.50 full cost and the fixed costs are irrelevant — they don't change. Accept the order.

② Make-or-buy (insourcing vs outsourcing)

Outsourcing = buying from outside; insourcing = producing internally. The most important factors: quality, dependability of supply, and cost. Gabriela needs 150,000 of Part #2; Alec offers £0.55 each.

Next year: 150 batches of 1,000 units; variable material-handling/set-up = £100/batch (so 150 × £100 = £15,000); fixed material-handling = £9,000. Variable manufacturing per unit = (DM 28,000 + DL 18,500 + variable OH 15,000) ÷ 150,000 = £0.41.

Scenario 1 — £9,000 fixed is unavoidable

Make: manufacturing (61,500) + handling/set-ups (15,000)£76,500
Buy: 150,000 × £0.55£82,500
Making saves£6,000 → MAKE

Scenario 2 — £9,000 fixed is avoidable if buying

Make: 76,500 + avoidable fixed 9,000£85,500
Buy£82,500
Buying saves£3,000 → BUY
The lesson

Whether a fixed cost is relevant depends on whether it's avoidable. The same problem flips from "make" to "buy" purely because £9,000 of fixed cost became avoidable.

Adding opportunity cost

If Gabriela buys Part #2, the freed facilities can make Part #3 for Krysta → +£18,000 profit. Compare the net cost of obtaining 150,000 Part #2:

OptionNet relevant cost
Make Part #2 (no Part #3)£76,500
Buy Part #2, don't make Part #3£82,500
Buy Part #2 & make Part #3£82,500 − £18,000 = £64,500 ✓ lowest

The £18,000 forgone profit is an opportunity cost of making in-house. Including it, Gabriela should buy Part #2 and use the space for Part #3.

③ Order-size / stock decision (opportunity cost of holding stock)

150,000 units needed. Order cost £40/order. Price £0.55 if ordered in 1,500s, £0.54 if all 150,000 at once. Money tied up in stock could earn 6%. Average investment in stock = (order value) ÷ 2.

Option A — 100 orders of 1,500

Order costs (100 × £40)£4,000.00
Purchase (150,000 × £0.55)£82,500.00
Interest forgone (£412.50 × 6%)£24.75
Relevant cost£86,524.75

Option B — 1 order of 150,000

Order cost (1 × £40)£40
Purchase (150,000 × £0.54)£81,000
Interest forgone (£40,500 × 6%)£2,430
Relevant cost£83,470 ✓

Buy all 150,000 at once: the lower price & ordering cost outweigh the higher opportunity cost of holding more stock.

④ Product mix under a capacity constraint

3,000 machine hours are the constraint. Which product to emphasise?

Per unitProduct #2Product #3
Contribution margin£1.70 (CM ratio 81%)£0.60 (CM ratio 4%)
Machine hours required7 hrs2 hrs
CM per machine hour£1.70 ÷ 7 = £0.24£0.60 ÷ 2 = £0.30 ✓
Decision rule

Maximise contribution margin per unit of the constraining resource — not CM per unit. Product #3 wins (£0.30 > £0.24) despite a far lower CM per unit and CM ratio, because it uses far fewer machine hours. With multiple constraints, use linear programming.

Bottlenecks & the Theory of Constraints (TOC)

A bottleneck limits the whole system's capacity (water leaving a bottle is limited by the neck). TOC's three measures: throughput margin, investments, operating costs — aim to increase throughput margin while decreasing investments & operating costs.

1Recognize the bottleneck sets the whole system's CM
→
2Identify the bottleneck

3Keep it busy; subordinate everything else to it
→
4Increase its efficiency & capacity

⑤ Customer profitability

Some customers lose money. Example operating income: Vogel €9,000, Brenner €16,000, Wisk €(32,000) → total €(7,000).

  • Why a customer is unprofitable: low-margin products ordered, high sales-order costs, high delivery/handling costs, high marketing costs. Work with them to reduce costs before dropping.
  • Dropping vs adding — the key distinction: depreciation on already-purchased equipment is a sunk cost → irrelevant when deciding to drop a customer; but the cost of new equipment to be bought (its future depreciation) IS relevant when deciding to add a customer.

The headline rule

The book value of existing equipment is irrelevant — it's a sunk cost (neither future nor different between alternatives). Irrelevant: cost, accumulated depreciation, book value of old machine, and any accounting gain/loss on disposal. Relevant: the current disposal value of the old machine and the purchase cost of the new one.

Performance-evaluation conflict

The decision model can say "replace," but a manager may resist if scrapping the old machine shows a loss that hurts their performance review. Ideally, design the decision model and the performance-evaluation model to be consistent.

Self-test

Q1For a special order with spare capacity, which costs are relevant?
Only the incremental variable costs of the order (and any avoidable costs). Fixed costs and full unit cost are irrelevant — accept if revenue > incremental cost.
Q2In make-or-buy, when does a fixed cost become relevant?
When it is avoidable under one alternative. Gabriela's decision flipped from MAKE (save £6,000) to BUY (save £3,000) once £9,000 of fixed cost became avoidable.
Q3With a single constraint, what do you maximise?
Contribution margin per unit of the constraining resource (e.g. CM per machine hour) — not CM per unit. Product #3 won at £0.30/hr vs Product #2 at £0.24/hr.
Q4Why is book value irrelevant in equipment replacement?
It's a sunk (past) cost — it's not a future cost and doesn't differ between keeping or replacing. Relevant items: old machine's current disposal value and the new machine's cost.
Q5Depreciation when dropping vs adding a customer?
Dropping: depreciation on equipment already owned is sunk → irrelevant. Adding: the cost of new equipment (future depreciation) is relevant.
Q6What are TOC's three measures and its goal?
Throughput margin, investments, operating costs. Goal: increase throughput margin while decreasing investments and operating costs; manage the bottleneck.
