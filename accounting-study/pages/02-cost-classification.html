Chapter 2 · Cost terms

How Costs Are Classified

The highest-value topic for quick marks. Master product vs period, the three product costs, direct vs indirect, and prime vs conversion — then drill the two worksheets.

Product costs vs period costs

Product (inventoriable) costs

Costs to make/buy the product. They attach to inventory and are expensed as Cost of Goods Sold only when the product is sold.

For a manufacturer: DM + DL + MOH.

Period (noninventoriable) costs

All selling and administrative costs. Expensed in the period incurred — never inventoried.

e.g. advertising, sales commissions, executive salaries, delivery to customers.

The deciding question

"Is this cost incurred inside the factory to make the product?" If yes → product cost. If it's selling or administrative (office, sales, delivery) → period cost.

The three product costs

Direct Materials (DM)

Raw materials used in production that can be traced to the product. e.g. chips in a calculator, yarn in a sweater.

Direct Labor (DL)

Labor of employees working on the products. e.g. workers assembling computers.

Manufacturing Overhead (MOH)

All indirect product costs: indirect materials, indirect labor, and factory rent, utilities, insurance, depreciation.

Direct vs indirect costs

Direct costs

Traced to a cost object. For a product: direct materials and direct labor.

Indirect costs

Not traced to a cost object — must be allocated. For a product: manufacturing overhead.

Prime costs & conversion costs

Prime costs

The direct costs: DM + DL.

Conversion costs

Costs to convert raw materials into finished goods: DL + MOH.

Memory hook

Direct Labor is in both — it's the overlap. Prime = DM + DL (the "primary"/direct inputs). Conversion = DL + MOH (what it takes to convert materials). Add DM + DL + MOH once and you get total product cost.

Two traps examiners love

Packaging: a box that holds the product the customer buys (cereal/detergent box) = product cost; packing for shipment = period cost. Factory facilities: lunchroom, soap, supervisors, property taxes inside the factory are all MOH = product; the same item in the head office is a period cost.

Self-test

Q1What three costs make up a manufacturer's product cost?
Direct Materials + Direct Labor + Manufacturing Overhead (DM + DL + MOH).
Q2Define prime cost and conversion cost.
Prime = DM + DL (the direct inputs). Conversion = DL + MOH (cost to convert materials to finished goods). DL is in both.
Q3When is a product cost actually expensed?
When the product is sold — it becomes Cost of Goods Sold. Until then it sits in inventory. (Period costs are expensed immediately.)
Q4Factory supervisor's salary vs sales manager's salary?
Factory supervisor = product (MOH, indirect labor). Sales manager = period (selling).
Q5Is the box around a product a product or period cost?
Packaging that holds the product the customer buys = product cost (direct material). Packing materials used only to ship goods = period cost.
