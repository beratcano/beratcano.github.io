Relevant Information for Decision Making

The single most important idea in the back half of the course: only future costs that differ between alternatives matter. Everything else — especially sunk costs — is noise.

A decision model is a formal method for making a choice, using quantitative and qualitative analysis.

What makes a cost relevant?

Definition

Relevant costs and revenues are expected FUTURE costs and revenues that DIFFER among alternative courses of action. Both tests must hold: it must be in the future, and it must differ between the options.

Relevant

  • Future
  • Differs between alternatives
  • e.g. the extra materials & labor to take a special order

Irrelevant

  • Historical / past costs — irrelevant to the decision (but used to predict future costs)
  • Sunk costs — past costs that are unavoidable; never differ, never relevant
  • Anything identical across the options

Differential & incremental — the vocabulary

TermMeaning
Incremental costThe additional total cost incurred for an activity
Differential costThe difference in total cost between two alternatives
Incremental revenueThe additional total revenue from an activity
Differential revenueThe difference in total revenue between two alternatives
Differential (net relevant) profitThe difference in total operating profit between two alternatives
Memory hook

"Future + Differs = Relevant." If a number is the same whichever way you decide, cross it out. Sunk costs are the classic trap — they're gone, so ignore them.

Quantitative vs qualitative factors

Quantitative factors

Outcomes measured in numerical terms — both financial (costs, revenues) and non-financial (e.g. defect rates, delivery times).

Qualitative factors

Outcomes that cannot be measured numerically — e.g. supplier reliability, employee morale, customer goodwill, quality reputation.

A good decision weighs both. The numbers can favour one option while qualitative factors (quality, dependability) point the other way.

Two pitfalls in relevant-cost analysis

1 · Bad general assumptions

  • Don't assume all variable costs are relevant.
  • Don't assume all fixed costs are irrelevant.

A fixed cost that is avoidable under one option IS relevant; a variable cost that's identical either way is NOT.

2 · Misleading unit-cost data

  • Unit costs can include irrelevant costs (e.g. allocated fixed overhead).
  • The same unit cost gets misapplied at different output levels.

Work with totals, not per-unit figures, when in doubt.

Opportunity cost

Definition

Opportunity cost is the contribution to profit that is foregone (rejected) by not using a limited resource in its next-best alternative use.

  • It is not recorded in formal accounting records — there's no cash outlay.
  • It is not in formal reports, yet it is genuinely relevant to decisions.
  • Example: the opportunity cost of holding stock is the profit forgone from tying up money in inventory instead of investing it elsewhere.
Why it matters

If accepting one option means giving up the profit from another (e.g. freeing factory space to make a different, profitable part), that lost profit is an opportunity cost you must include — even though no accountant ever books it. You'll apply this in the make-or-buy example in Chapter 5.

Self-test

Q1State the two-part test for a relevant cost.
It must be (1) a future cost/revenue and (2) one that differs between the alternatives. Both must hold.
Q2What is a sunk cost and is it ever relevant?
A past cost that is unavoidable. It never differs between alternatives, so it is never relevant.
Q3List the five steps of the decision process.
Gather information → make predictions → choose an alternative → implement → evaluate performance.
Q4Are all fixed costs irrelevant? Are all variable costs relevant?
No to both. An avoidable fixed cost is relevant; a variable cost that's the same under every option is irrelevant. Judge each cost by "future + differs."
Q5Define opportunity cost and note where it appears in the books.
The profit forgone from not using a limited resource in its next-best use. It is not recorded in the accounting records (no cash outlay) but is still relevant.
