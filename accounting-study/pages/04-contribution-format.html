Chapter 4 · Cost behavior

Contribution Format Income Statement

The same profit, sliced a smarter way: organise costs by behavior (variable vs fixed) instead of by function — the foundation for every decision in the back half of the course.

What it is

The contribution format (also called the variable costing income statement) reorganises the income statement around cost behavior instead of around function.

Traditional / functional format

Groups costs as COGS, then selling & admin. Key subtotal = Gross Profit. Used for external reporting.

Contribution format

Groups all variable costs first, then all fixed costs. Key subtotal = Contribution Margin. Used for internal decisions & CVP analysis.

Both reach the same Net Income

The two formats arrive at identical net income — they just slice the costs differently (behavior vs function).

The layout & two clean steps

Salesxxx
(−) Variable costs: production + general & admin + marketing(xx)
(=) Contribution Marginxxx
(−) Fixed costs: production + general & admin + marketing(x)
(=) Net Incomexx

Step 1

Sales − ALL variable costs = Contribution Margin

Step 2

Contribution Margin − ALL fixed costs = Net Income

Key idea: Contribution Margin

Definition

Contribution Margin (CM) = Sales − Variable Costs. It's what's "left over" after covering all variable costs — available to cover fixed costs and then contribute to profit.

  • If CM > Fixed Costs → profit (positive net income)
  • If CM = Fixed Costs → break-even (net income = 0)
  • If CM < Fixed Costs → loss

Unit Contribution Margin

= Selling price per unit − Variable cost per unit

Contribution Margin Ratio

= Contribution Margin ÷ Sales

The crucial sorting rule

Every cost is split on two questions at once:

QuestionWhat it decides
Is it variable or fixed?Whether it sits above CM (variable) or below CM (fixed) — the block it goes in
What function? (production / gen-admin / marketing)The line label only
The trap

Behavior comes first, function second. A sales commission per unit is a variable marketing cost → it sits in the variable block (even though it's "selling"). Factory rent is a fixed production cost → it sits in the fixed block (even though it's "production"). This is the opposite cut from the traditional format, where all production costs land in COGS.

Worked sketch

Sales 100,000 · Variable: production 30,000, admin 5,000, marketing 10,000 · Fixed: production 20,000, admin 8,000, marketing 7,000.

Sales100,000
(−) Variable costs (30,000 + 5,000 + 10,000)(45,000)
Contribution Margin55,000
(−) Fixed costs (20,000 + 8,000 + 7,000)(35,000)
Net Income20,000

CM ratio = 55,000 ÷ 100,000 = 55%. Practise the full Vega & Atlas problems in the Practice chapter.

Memory hook

Contribution = behavior-first. Variables up top → Contribution Margin → fixed below → Net Income. Gross profit is the traditional subtotal; contribution margin is the contribution-format subtotal.

Self-test

Q1What does the contribution format organise costs by, and what's its key subtotal?
By cost behavior (variable vs fixed); the key subtotal is Contribution Margin (vs Gross Profit in the traditional format).
Q2Define contribution margin and the CM ratio.
CM = Sales − Variable Costs (what's left to cover fixed costs & profit). CM ratio = CM ÷ Sales. Unit CM = price − variable cost per unit.
Q3Where does a per-unit sales commission go, and why?
In the variable block (it's a variable marketing cost). Behavior decides the block; function is just the label.
Q4Do the traditional and contribution formats give the same net income?
Yes — identical net income; only the grouping and subtotals differ.
