Business Processes & the ERP Process Approach

Moving from functional silos to an integrated, process-oriented architecture — plus the reference models and BPMN notation used to map it all.

What is a business process?

Definition

A structured sequence of interrelated activities that transforms a specific input into a specific output in order to deliver value.

INPUTcustomer order, raw-material request, financial transaction, employee record
→
TRANSFORMATIONactivities, rules & controls, system support, human decisions
→
OUTPUTproduct/service, report/document, approval/rejection, customer satisfaction

Hammer & Champy (1993): a business process is a collection of activities that takes one or more kinds of input and creates an output of value to the customer. (Test: "Is buying a coffee in the morning a process?")

From functional silos to a process-oriented structure

Functional structure (before ERP)

Departments: Sales, Finance, Production, HR, Logistics.

  • Information flow fragmented across departments
  • Customer orders re-entered in each department
  • Accountability gaps between functions

Process-oriented structure (after ERP)

Processes: Order-to-Cash, Procure-to-Pay, Production Planning, Hire-to-Retire.

  • End-to-end process ownership
  • Real-time data sharing across functions
  • One record flows through the whole chain

Cross-functional processes in ERP

Each process runs through real-time integration of multiple modules. Data is entered once and consumed across all relevant modules.

Reference process models

Standard frameworks that define industry best practices used in ERP implementations.

SCOR

Supply Chain Operations Reference — org: ASCM (formerly APICS). Standardizes supply-chain processes as Plan–Source–Make–Deliver–Return–Enable. Use: logistics, manufacturing, supplier optimization.

APQC PCF

Process Classification Framework — org: APQC. Cross-industry architecture: 1,000+ process definitions across 12 categories. Use: benchmarking & improvement prioritization.

ARIS

Architecture of Integrated Information Systems — org: Software AG. Models from 5 perspectives: organization, data, function, output, control. Use: process design/documentation in SAP.

Mnemonic

SCOR = supply-chain "P-S-M-D-R-E". ARIS = 5 views ("ODFOC": Organization, Data, Function, Output, Control). APQC = 12 categories, 1000+ processes.

Process mapping & BPMN fundamentals

BPMN = Business Process Model and Notation: a standardized graphical language for modeling business processes, maintained by the OMG.

SymbolElementMeaning
●Start EventWhere the process begins
○End EventWhere the process ends
▭TaskAn activity — automated (■) or manual (□)
◆GatewayA decision; ⊕ parallel gateway = split/merge parallel paths
[ ]SubprocessA collapsed nested process
→Sequence FlowOrder of activities (-- message flow = across participants)

Why use process maps?

Visibility — makes complex processes visual.
Analysis — exposes bottlenecks, redundancies, gaps, accountability issues.
ERP design — clarifies which step belongs to which module.
Training — new users grasp the process; error rates drop.
Continuous improvement — enables AS-IS vs TO-BE analysis.
Worksheet pattern (AS-IS → TO-BE)

The BPMN exercise scenarios (onboarding, complaint handling, purchase requisition) all follow the same logic: map the current (AS-IS) process with swimlanes per actor, find the delays/handoffs, then design a TO-BE where ERP automates steps. Key questions: which steps can ERP trigger automatically? and which approvals genuinely add value?

The customization decision: standardization vs customization

One of the most critical implementation decisions: adapt your process to the ERP, or adapt the ERP to your process?

Standardization ("Vanilla" ERP)

+ Lower initial cost · easier upgrades/patches · industry best practices · reduced risk · lower long-term maintenance.

− Existing processes must change · hard to differentiate competitively · adoption takes time.

Customization

+ Competitive processes preserved · lower user resistance · industry-specific needs met.

− High development cost · complex upgrades · technical debt accumulates · vendor lock-in risk.

Golden rule

"Try the standard first." Fit the process to the system, not the system to the process. (This rule comes back hard in Chapter 8.)
