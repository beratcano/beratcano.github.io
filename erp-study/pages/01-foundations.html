ERP Foundations

The "integration + process + data" logic of ERP, where it sits in the enterprise, and the vocabulary the rest of the course is built on.

The one-line definition

ERP is a suite of enterprise applications that runs a company's core processes using shared data and rules, integrating functions across the organization. The motto: "enter once, use everywhere."

What ERP actually is

  • Purpose — "enter once, use everywhere": single, consistent data (one source of truth).
  • Process-oriented: follows an end-to-end flow, not departments.
  • Standard business rules + traceability: who changed what, when?
  • Real-time reporting and an audit trail.
Exam framing

ERP is not a single product — it's an ecosystem and an architectural decision. It creates value through integration and data, not through any one screen.

Why ERP? The problems it solves

Pre-ERP environment

  • Departmental systems (software + Excel + email)
  • Same data in many places → inconsistent inventory/customer records
  • Manual approvals and delays
  • Unclear which data is "the truth"
  • Reporting = a "collect → merge → clean" cycle

The ERP promise

  • Shared data model & a single source of truth
  • Process standardization and automation
  • Integration: order → inventory → invoice → accounting
  • Traceability and control (audit trail)
  • Faster, more consistent decision support

Where ERP fits — the enterprise application landscape

ERP sits at the heart of core business processes inside a layered architecture (top → bottom):

Users / Channelsweb, mobile, dealers, e-commerce — the interface
Business ApplicationsERP, CRM, SCM, HCM — the system of record
IntegrationAPI, EDI, ESB/iPaaS — work/data flow
Data Layermaster data, transactional data, data warehouse — analytics
Infrastructurecloud / on-premise, security, monitoring

ERP lives mainly in the Business Applications layer but creates value through integration and data.

The 4 core principles of ERP

① Integration

Modules work on the same data.

② Standard process

End-to-end flow and rules.

③ Single source of data

Master data + audit trail (e.g. the product card).

④ Control / reporting

Real-time visibility.

Mnemonic

"I S S C" → Integration, Standard process, Single source, Control. The "4 core principles."

End-to-end (E2E) processes

The best way to learn ERP is to follow processes end-to-end:

Most important point

Most "problems" come not from a module itself, but from cross-module integration and data management.

Data: master vs transactional

Master data

  • Relatively stable, used across many processes
  • Examples: customer, supplier, product/service, warehouse, pricing
  • The infrastructure; needs ownership & approval
  • Poor master data → errors in every module

Transactional data

  • Created by day-to-day operations
  • Examples: order, delivery note, inventory movement, invoice, payment
  • Shows process flow & performance (the proof)
  • Foundation for reporting and auditing
The rule

Without correct master data, you cannot produce "good" transactional data.

A single sales order creates a chain reaction across modules:

Performance: the KPI logic

The real value of ERP is making process performance measurable — "How long did it take? Where did they wait? Who approved it?"

O2C KPIs

  • Order cycle time
  • Shipping accuracy / OTIF (on-time-in-full)
  • Billing delay
  • DSO (Days Sales Outstanding) / collection time

P2P KPIs

  • Lead time / delivery performance
  • Inventory turnover
  • Purchasing savings (price variance)
  • Invoice match rate (PO-GR-Invoice)
Chain to remember

KPI definition → data definition → process standard → system design. They're all connected: if not everyone posts their shipment on time in the system, the KPI is meaningless.

Benefits vs risks

Typical benefits

  • Data consistency (single customer/product/warehouse records)
  • Standardization & control of processes
  • Operational visibility (orders, inventory, production, cash)
  • Traceability & compliance (audit trail)
  • Faster close and reporting
  • Risk reduction via authorization & segregation of duties (SoD)

Typical risks

Non-technical (more common!):

  • Change management / user resistance
  • Scope creep
  • Unclear process ownership
  • Insufficient training & adoption
  • Data cleansing / migration difficulty

Technical / architectural:

  • Poor integration design; performance/scalability
  • Authorization mistakes; customization dependency; reporting gaps
Realistic expectation

ERP is not a miracle. It creates value with correct data, correct processes, and good change management — and most risk is human/process/project, not technology.

Cloud ERP or on-prem?

Cloud

  • Faster deployment / scaling
  • More frequent (regular) updates
  • Less infrastructure burden
  • Encourages alignment with standard processes (less customization)

On-prem

  • More control / customization
  • Infrastructure & maintenance responsibility
  • Updates can be heavier
  • Regulatory / integration needs can be decisive

Course view: choose on logic, not brand — process fit, data, integration, total cost of ownership.

Security & authorization (basics)

  • Role-based access: user → role → permission.
  • Segregation of duties (SoD): the same person should not both order and pay.
  • Approval workflows: limits and levels.
  • Audit trail: who changed what, when?
  • Authorization design is part of business risk management.

Reporting: operational vs analytical

Operational reports

  • Daily operational tracking
  • Current inventory, open orders, pending invoices
  • Transaction-level detail (drill-down)
  • Helps catch process issues

Analytical reports (BI)

  • Trends, profitability, performance
  • KPI dashboards
  • Summary + multidimensional analysis
  • Needs a data warehouse / modeling

Self-test

Q1Give the one-line definition of ERP and its motto.
A suite of enterprise applications that runs a company's core processes using shared data and rules, integrating functions across the organization. Motto: "enter once, use everywhere."
Q2Name the four core principles of ERP.
Integration, Standard process, Single source of data, Control/reporting.
Q3What are the steps of O2C and P2P?
O2C: Quotation → Order → Shipment → Invoice → Collection. P2P: Requisition → Order → Goods Receipt → Invoice → Payment.
Q4Master data vs transactional data — give two examples of each.
Master (stable): customer, supplier, material, pricing. Transactional (daily ops): order, invoice, inventory movement, payment. Rule: no good transactional data without correct master data.
Q5Where do most ERP problems and most ERP risk come from?
Problems come from cross-module integration & data management (not a single module). Most risk is non-technical: change management, scope creep, unclear ownership, training/adoption.
Q6What is segregation of duties (SoD)? Give the classic example.
A control where one person can't perform conflicting tasks — classic example: the same person should not both order and pay. Part of role-based access & business risk management.
Q7What does the history MRP → MRP II → ERP add at each step?
MRP = material needs (what/how many/when). MRP II = adds capacity feasibility. ERP = integrates all core functions on shared data. ERP+ = ecosystem with CRM/SCM/BI, cloud, mobile, analytics.
