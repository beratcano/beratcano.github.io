Chapter 9 · Readings 1 & 2

Why ERP Projects Succeed or Fail

Two readings: a review of ERP/enterprise-IT failure cases (Salazar-Fierro et al., 2023) and a content analysis of success vs failure factors in 44 SAP implementations (Gargeya & Brady, 2005). Great material for short-answer and essay questions.

Reading 1 — Failures in enterprise IT applications

An EITA (Enterprise IT Application) is a ready-to-use package — ERP, CRM, LMS, PoS (a.k.a. COTS / RUSP). The paper uses "implantation" (medical metaphor: inserting an external artifact into the body) deliberately.

What counts as failure?

Cancellation, inability to run the organization after go-live, disruption of production/workflow, or a system with inadequate features. Four classifications:

Correspondence failure — doesn't meet the initially designed objectives.
Process failure — not produced within budget & schedule.
Interaction failure — users don't use it / low satisfaction.
Expectation failure — fails to meet stakeholders' expectations.
Failure rates (cited)

Studies report failure rates between 30% and 70%: ~57% take longer than expected, 54% exceed budget, 41% miss >50% of benefits. Only ~50% of ERP implementations are successful.

Common failure factors (across studies)

Poor consultant effectiveness · poor BPR quality · poor project management · ERP software misfit · high team turnover · over-reliance on heavy customization · poor knowledge transfer · unrealistic top-management expectations · schedule too tight · user resistance to change · poor top-management support · poor testing · data quality. Grouped: cultural, business, and technical problems — and they recur across the pre-, during-, and post-implementation phases.

Pattern

Failure is not mainly technical — it's coordination & management: tight schedules, bad go-live timing, scope/requirements gaps, customization over process change, weak change management, and executive turnover. (SAP itself "performs as advertised.")

Reading 2 — Gargeya & Brady (2005): success vs failure factors

Content analysis of published articles on 44 companies that implemented SAP (1995–2000): 29 successes, 15 failures. They identified 6 factor groups. (Gartner context: ~70% of ERP projects fail to be fully implemented even after 3 years; SAP held >50% market share.)

FactorSuccess (n=29)Failure (n=15)
1 · Worked with SAP functionality / maintained scope55.17%6.67%
2 · Project team / management support / consultants48.28%6.67%
3 · Internal readiness / training41.38%53.33%
4 · Deal with organizational diversity31.03%13.33%
5 · Planning / development / budgeting20.69%40.00%
6 · Adequate testing10.34%20.00%
The headline finding

The factors that drive success are not the mirror image of those that drive failure:

  • Top success factors: working with SAP functionality & maintaining scope; strong project team / management support.
  • Top failure factors: inadequate internal readiness & training; poor planning & budgeting.

So management should focus on one set of factors to avoid failure and another set to ensure success.

What each factor means

  • 1. SAP functionality / scope — adopt "vanilla" SAP, clean up operations, minimal customization, control scope creep, choose Big-Bang vs phased rollout deliberately (Chevron used phased to replace 250+ legacy systems).
  • 2. Project team / management support — cross-functional team of the best people, dedicated full-time; committed senior executives (funding, incentives, communication).
  • 3. Internal readiness / training — the "people element," historically under-funded; the most common failure factor ("readiness for change"). Unisource scuttled its project over cultural change.
  • 4. Organizational diversity — large/global firms must re-engineer culture & processes (Amoco, Chevron succeeded; E.I. DuPont scuttled over excessive diversity).
  • 5. Planning / development / budgeting — cost overruns & delays are fatal; only ~1/6 of projects finish on time & on budget; some run 189% over.
  • 6. Adequate testing — Gillette tested 5 months before go-live; Whirlpool ignored "red flags" to hit a deadline and got costly inventory/delivery problems (a one-week test delay would have prevented it).
Quotable conclusions

The biggest regret is too little attention to internal readiness & change. "Go-live is not the brass ring" — it's a milestone toward the real goal: realizing the benefits. Factors are intertwined; you need the whole system, not one criterion.

Self-test

Q1Name the four failure classifications from Reading 1.
Correspondence, Process, Interaction, Expectation failures.
Q2What caused Lidl's ~€500M failure?
A requirements gap: SAP based inventory on retail prices, Lidl used purchase prices — they customized instead of changing the process; plus 7-year duration and executive turnover.
Q3What's the common thread across Hershey and J&J Snack Foods?
Both cut over at a bad time (peak/busy season) with compressed schedules and reduced testing — timing & planning failures, not software.
Q4In Gargeya & Brady, what are the top success vs top failure factors?
Success: working with SAP functionality/maintaining scope (55%) and project team/management support (48%). Failure: inadequate internal readiness/training (53%) and poor planning/budgeting (40%).
Q5What is the headline conclusion of Gargeya & Brady?
Success factors are not the same as failure factors — manage one set to avoid failure and a different set to ensure success. The most under-attended factor is internal readiness/change.
Q6What did Whirlpool do wrong?
Cut testing to meet a deadline and ignored "red flags" → costly inventory/delivery problems at go-live. A one-week test delay would have prevented it (also shows weak top-management support for testing).
